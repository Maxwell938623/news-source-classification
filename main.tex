\section{Introduction}
In this project, we built an end-to-end binary NLP classification pipeline to predict whether a headline came from Fox News or NBC News. Our main goal was to make the system reproducible and easily extensible, and so every major stage (URL collection, scraping, preprocessing, splitting, training, evaluation, and prediction) is modularized as its own script.

We first reproduced a simple reference baseline (TF-IDF capped at 100 terms plus logistic regression with a 100-iteration limit), then expanded to a controlled experiment framework covering multiple feature and model families based on what we thought would perform well in the context of this experiment. Our training framework evaluates a large configuration space and selects the best model by validation macro-F1 before retraining on train+validation and evaluating once on the held-out test set.

\section{Core Components}

\subsection{Data Collection and Dataset}
The pipeline runs in two stages. \textbf{URL discovery}: a collector traverses robots-discovered sitemap indexes, configured sitemap seeds, RSS/Atom feeds, and section pages for FoxNews and NBC, kept running for seven consecutive days to maximise historical coverage; collection stays within publicly advertised article URLs, respects robots.txt, and uses concurrency with exponential backoff. \textbf{Headline scraping}: each URL is fetched with retry/backoff and headlines are extracted via a prioritised fallback chain --- source-specific tag heuristics, Open Graph/Schema.org fields, the first generic page heading, and finally the document title --- so per-template variation does not break the system. URL discovery yielded 172{,}488 URLs (115{,}266 FoxNews; 57{,}222 NBC), each labelled by publisher domain. Preprocessing drops rows with missing or conflicting labels and failed scrapes, performs HTML cleanup, whitespace normalisation, intra- and inter-source deduplication, and label validation, producing a merged pool of 114{,}306 successfully scraped headlines.

For the classical experiments we built a 20{,}000-headline curated dataset by anchoring on the provided helper CSV and drawing a fixed-seed random sample from the larger scrape, excluding duplicate helper URLs and headlines and targeting approximate source balance. The cleaned table has 10{,}513 FoxNews (class 0) and 9{,}487 NBC (class 1) headlines averaging $\approx$13.2 words. Stratified 70/15/15 splits with seed 42 give 13{,}999 train / 3{,}000 validation / 3{,}001 test examples, drawn at random within class rather than chronologically; this simplifies variance estimation but can introduce topic drift relative to the leaderboard window, which we discuss in §2.4.

\subsection{Model Design}
We swept sparse classical models, dense embedding heads, ensemble combinations, and fine-tuned transformer classifiers on the shared headline splits. Throughout, FoxNews is class 0 and NBC class 1, splits are stratified with seed 42, hyperparameters are chosen by validation macro-F1, and the selected model is refit on train+validation and scored once on the held-out test set.

\textbf{Baseline:} TF-IDF (100 features) with logistic regression. \textbf{Classical and dense families:} word TF-IDF with logistic regression / linear SVM / multinomial naive Bayes; character n-gram TF-IDF with linear heads (incl.\ fused word--character pipelines); stylometric features with LR, SVM, random forest, or HistGradientBoosting; hybrid sparse inputs combining word TF-IDF, character TF-IDF, and stylometry; soft-voting ensembles over calibrated bases; stacking ensembles with a meta-classifier over base-model probabilities; gradient boosted trees over TF-IDF plus stylometry; sentence-transformer embeddings with linear, MLP, LightGBM, or XGBoost heads; supervised fastText; averaged static embeddings with a classifier head. \textbf{Fine-tuned transformers:} DistilBERT-base uncased, DeBERTa-v3-small, ModernBERT-base.

Four leaderboard packages live under \texttt{submission/}, all sharing the \texttt{model.py + preprocess.py + model.pt} contract:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{distilbert\_model/} --- fine-tuned DistilBERT-base uncased (top test macro-F1 in Table~\ref{tab:results}).
    \item \texttt{modernbert\_model/} --- fine-tuned ModernBERT-base sequence classifier.
    \item \texttt{stack\_4base\_histgbm\_model/} --- four-base stacking ensemble with HistGradientBoosting meta-learner (best sparse pipeline), serialised as a scikit-learn \texttt{Pipeline} embedded inside a torch \texttt{state\_dict}.
    \item \texttt{gbdt\_model/} --- gradient boosted trees over TF-IDF + stylometry (compact reference).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation and Model Performance}
We report accuracy, macro-F1, per-class F1, confusion matrices, and (when available) ROC summaries as diagnostics; model selection itself tracks validation macro-F1. Held-out scores are in Table~\ref{tab:results}; selection validation macro-F1 was 0.8004 (HistGBM stack), 0.7727 (GBDT hybrid), and 0.8218 (ModernBERT). Per-class F1 on the 3{,}001-example test fold: baseline FoxNews 0.6736 / NBC 0.5588; HistGBM stack 0.8018 / 0.7893; GBDT hybrid 0.7721 / 0.7518; ModernBERT 0.8284 / 0.8078 on its 3{,}000-example fold.

Relative to baseline on the 20k split, the HistGBM stack lifts test accuracy by 17.1 points (0.6248$\to$0.7957) and macro-F1 by 17.9 points; DistilBERT adds another $\approx$3.1 macro-F1 points on top. ModernBERT-base reaches 0.8181 macro-F1, slightly below DistilBERT/DeBERTa here but still competitive. (Our baseline of 0.6248 is on the 3{,}001-example test fold of the 20k curated set; the same vectoriser+LogReg configuration on smaller splits gives different absolute numbers but the same ordering across rows of Table~\ref{tab:results}.)

\subsection{Error Analysis and Limitations}
We expected remaining errors to concentrate on linguistically shallow headlines (few tokens, generic ledes) and on politically overloaded wording shared by both outlets --- producing roughly symmetric confusion off-diagonals for strong models and low-confidence mistakes in sparse pipelines. Held-out confusion matrices and Figure~\ref{fig:results_visual} bear this out.

\textbf{Per-class breakdown} (on the 3{,}001-example test fold; 1{,}577 Fox / 1{,}424 NBC unless noted): the HistGBM stack misclassifies 337 Fox-as-NBC (21.4\%) and 276 NBC-as-Fox (19.4\%) --- balanced sampling kills the extreme class-asymmetry seen in earlier large-corpus runs, leaving errors driven by headline ambiguity rather than class prior. ModernBERT (3{,}000-example fold; 1{,}577 / 1{,}423): 264 Fox-as-NBC (16.8\%) and 280 NBC-as-Fox (19.7\%). GBDT hybrid: 369 / 1{,}577 (23.4\%) and 344 / 1{,}424 (24.2\%). \textbf{Failure modes}: short/generic headlines carry little lexical or stylistic signal; politics-heavy headlines share vocabulary across outlets (major candidates, elections) and collapse calibrated probabilities toward 0.5; URL slug fallbacks still hurt when a leaderboard URL cannot be scraped cleanly.

\textbf{Limitations.} The 20k dataset is one random draw from the larger scrape, so coverage is broad but not exhaustive; our local test predates leaderboard release, so post-deadline topic drift is unmeasured here; transformer fine-tuning uses fixed max sequence lengths and clips rare long headlines; we train on headlines only --- full article bodies are out of scope.

\section{Exploratory Components}

\textbf{Code and engineering.} Scraping at scale is fragile unless ingestion is restartable, observable, and gentle on upstream sites. A continuous-scrape driver chains URL discovery, CSV merges, and resumed headline extraction with structured logs, so nightly jobs replace manual copy-paste. The scraper layers source-specific tag heuristics over Open Graph/Schema.org/title fallbacks because Fox and NBC pages diverge in markup --- layering reduced blank-headline rates relative to any single DOM selector. Duplicate URLs reappear across sitemap refreshes, so merges deduplicate on canonical URLs before labelling; exponential backoff with capped parallelism stabilised week-long runs. The pipeline raised usable scraped rows to six figures and kept collection reproducible enough to regenerate the 20k study split from the same scripts.

\textbf{Techniques and literature grounding.} Word TF-IDF with sublinear TF emphasises discriminative tokens without dense storage; character n-grams capture morphology that survives noisy SEO-style headlines (cf.\ noisy-channel spelling models). Stylometric features --- punctuation balance, casing, length statistics --- borrow from authorship attribution but are tuned to outlet-level cues. Stacking diversifies errors across word/character/stylometry views; meta-learners are trained on out-of-fold probabilities, approximating Wolpert-style stacking without leaking validation folds into selection. Dense sentence embeddings provided the next lift once sparse stacks plateaued, and transformer fine-tuning lifted again by conditioning on bidirectional context for ambiguous political wording. Macro-F1 is the selection metric because it weights both classes equally; calibrated SVM heads feed ensembles because raw margins lack probabilities.

\textbf{Dataset expansion.} Per-outlet backfill state lets historical URL lists grow outward without repeatedly scraping only the freshest RSS window; every enlargement flows through the same preprocessing contract from §2.1, so the curated 20k dataset can be regrown on demand from the underlying scrape pool of $\approx$114k headlines.

\textbf{Analysis.} §2.4 formalises the per-class outcome of the analysis thread: errors concentrate where lexical overlap dominates stylistic signal, transformers shrink but do not erase those ambiguity pockets, and balancing the source mix collapses prior-driven asymmetry into ambiguity-driven mistakes.

\textbf{Sparse pipelines vs.\ contextual encoders.} Sparse pipelines remain attractive for latency and interpretability --- TF-IDF coefficients name the influential n-grams, and stacking exposes where stylometry compensates for vocabulary overlap --- but they collapse toward 0.5 when outlets echo identical entities on breaking news. Transformers lift accuracy on those ambiguous cases (Table~\ref{tab:results}) at heavier deployment cost; DistilBERT tops our headline split, ModernBERT remains within 1 macro-F1 point. Empirically, transformer classifiers beat our strongest handcrafted pipeline by $\approx$3 macro-F1 points; classical stacks remain a useful low-latency fallback.
